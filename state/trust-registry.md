# Trust Registry — Who and What to Believe

## First Principle — the epistemic stance (Mohammad's rule)
The system trusts no one, and at the same time assumes no one is lying.
Every piece of information is EVIDENCE to be weighed, not truth to swallow or a lie to dismiss.
In practice:
- Nothing is acted on unverified — even Tier 1 gets sanity checks (dates fresh? numbers plausible?).
- Nothing is ignored purely because of its source — a Tier 4 rumor can PROMPT a Tier 1 verification;
  it just can never BE the verification. If a YouTube guru says a company is in trouble, we don't trade
  on his word and we don't assume he's wrong — we check the filings.
- Confidence scales with the source's accountability and our own graded track record of that source,
  never with how loud, famous, or confident the speaker is.
- The system extends the same skepticism to itself: its own filters and committee are graded in the
  shadow ledger, because we don't blindly trust us either.

Rule of the system: FACTS from Tier 1, CONTEXT from Tier 2, OPINIONS from nobody.
A trade may only ever be triggered by Tier 1 verifiable data. Tier 3-4 content can never trigger a trade.

## Tier 1 — Primary sources (trust the facts, they are legally accountable)
- SEC filings: EDGAR, 13F holdings (13f.info), Form 4 insider trades, 10-K/10-Q reports
- Official congressional disclosures (what Capitol Trades republishes)
- Company earnings releases and audited financial statements
- Federal Reserve / central bank statements
- Exchange data: actual prices, volumes (Yahoo Finance, TradingView data feeds)
Why trusted: lying in these is a crime, not a bad take.

## Tier 2 — Professional journalism (trust the reporting, watch the framing)
- Reuters, Bloomberg, Associated Press — fastest and most disciplined
- Wall Street Journal, Financial Times — deep reporting; separate news pages from opinion pages
Why trusted: professional standards, editors, retractions when wrong. Still: headlines are written to be clicked.

## Tier 3 — Analysis and opinion (useful for ideas, verify everything, never act directly)
- The Economist, Barron's, Morningstar research
- CNBC / financial TV: information mixed with entertainment; guests often talk their own book
- Seeking Alpha, Substack analysts: quality varies wildly per author, check their track record
- Sell-side analyst ratings: chronically optimistic (buy ratings vastly outnumber sells; banks want the companies' business)

## Tier 4 — Noise and predators (never trade on, assume marketing until proven otherwise)
- YouTube trading gurus, TikTok/Instagram traders
- Discord/Telegram signal groups, paid "communities" (Whop etc.)
- Reddit tickers hype, X/Twitter fintwit calls
- Anyone whose income is selling courses/signals rather than trading

## How to spot who is full of it — the checklist
1. Sells a course, community, or signals -> their product is YOU, not their trading
2. Shows P&L screenshots but no audited/broker-verified track record over years
3. Only shows winners; losers vanish silently
4. Urgency: "buy now", "last chance", "before it's too late"
5. Guarantees or "can't lose" language -> in markets this is always a lie
6. "Secret method they don't want you to know" -> there are no secrets in public markets
7. Vague credentials ("ex-Wall Street") doing retail YouTube -> real edge doesn't need ad revenue
8. Affiliate links to brokers/platforms -> they earn when you sign up, not when you profit
9. Track record impossible to falsify (paper trades, hindsight calls, deleted bad calls)
10. Lifestyle marketing (rented Lambos, beach laptops) -> traders show statements, marketers show lifestyles

## Which TRADERS to trust
Trust track records, not talk:
- Verifiable via SEC filings over decades: Buffett, and the 13F managers this system already follows
- Audited fund returns over 15+ years: trustworthy evidence (whether their style suits copying is separate)
- Everyone else, including every influencer: assume unproven. The system's own shadow ledger and source
  grading IS our trust mechanism — trust is earned inside our data, not claimed on social media.

## Standing rule for the agent
When gathering news (daily Step 1), prefer Tier 1-2 sources. If a signal or narrative originates from
Tier 3-4, it may be noted as sentiment context but must NEVER trigger, size, or veto a trade by itself.
